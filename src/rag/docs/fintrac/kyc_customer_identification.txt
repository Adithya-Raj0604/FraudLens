FINTRAC GUIDANCE: Know Your Customer (KYC) and Customer Identification
Source: PCMLTFA s.6, Client Identification Methods (2023 Update)
Last Updated: June 2023

WHEN IDENTIFICATION IS REQUIRED
Financial entities must identify clients when: opening an account, conducting a cash transaction of $10,000 CAD or more, conducting an international EFT of $1,000 CAD or more, when there are reasonable grounds to suspect money laundering, or when the transaction involves a politically exposed person (PEP).

IDENTIFICATION METHODS — INDIVIDUALS
Acceptable methods for identifying an individual include: (1) government-issued photo ID with name, date of birth, and unique identifier; (2) dual-process method combining two reliable sources (e.g., a utility bill + credit file); (3) credit file confirmation from a Canadian credit bureau; or (4) confirmation from a financial institution with which the client has an existing account.

BENEFICIAL OWNERSHIP
For entities (corporations, trusts, partnerships), the institution must identify all beneficial owners who directly or indirectly own or control 25% or more of the entity. This requires collecting full legal name, date of birth, address, and occupation for each beneficial owner. Beneficial ownership records must be updated at minimum every 3 years or when a material change is detected.

MULE ACCOUNT RED FLAGS
The following patterns during account opening or early usage are indicative of mule account activity and must trigger enhanced due diligence:
- Account opened with minimal documentation and funded immediately via large TRANSFER from an unknown source
- Transaction velocity exceeding 5 outbound transfers within the first 30 days of account opening
- Destination balance of $0.00 before and after receiving funds (funds passed through immediately)
- Origin balance draining to exactly $0.00 post-transfer with no corresponding business purpose
- IP geolocation inconsistent with stated residential address

ONGOING MONITORING
Institutions must conduct ongoing monitoring of business relationships to detect changes in risk profile. For high-risk accounts, monitoring must occur at minimum quarterly. Transaction monitoring systems must flag accounts where the cumulative 24-hour transfer volume exceeds the established baseline by more than 3 standard deviations.

RECORD RETENTION FOR KYC
KYC records must be retained for 5 years after the account is closed or the business relationship ends. For transactions, records must be kept for 5 years from the transaction date. All KYC documents must be stored in a retrievable format and made available to FINTRAC within 3 hours during an on-site examination.
